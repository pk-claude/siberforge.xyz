MSFT, GOOGL, META, AMZN — the customers paying for it all. Quarterly capex prints, AI-attributable revenue lift, and the ROIC question (when does the spending pay off, or stop?).

Planned: Q3 2026

PILLAR 3

Power & grid

CEG, VST, NEE, GEV, ETR — the constraint nobody priced in. Data center power demand, nuclear restart timelines, transmission build-out, PPA pricing for hyperscalers.

PILLAR 4

Adopters & second derivatives

Software (CRM, NOW, ADBE, PANW), services (ACN, IBM), industrial automation (ROK, EMR), and enterprises measurably saving cost from AI deployment. Where productivity actually shows up in the P&L.

CROSS-CUTTING

How this connects to the macro view

The Regime page treats sectors as base-rate plays. AI Beneficiaries layers on a structural-tilt overlay: which sectors get re-rated regardless of the macro regime because the AI capex cycle is exogenous to the standard growth/inflation framework. The Sample Window toggle on the Positioning section already addresses part of this — Post-2022 returns reflect the AI era. AI Beneficiaries goes further: per-name picks, per-pillar capex tracking, valuation sanity checks against historical capex booms (telecom 1999, fiber 2000, cloud 2014).

If you have specific picks, capex data sources, or framing arguments you want incorporated, drop them in the issues backlog. Build order is shaped by data availability — Compute and Hyperscalers ship first because the data is clean (10-Q capex, FactSet consensus). Power & grid is harder (PPA pricing is opaque). Adopters is hardest (productivity attribution is genuinely contested).

AI Beneficiaries is a working theory in progress, not a model. Updated alongside the macro views.
